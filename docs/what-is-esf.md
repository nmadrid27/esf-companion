# What Is the Epistemic Stewardship Framework?

ESF is a structured method for maintaining intellectual ownership when you work with AI. It gives you specific practices, checkpoints, and documentation so that the work AI helps you produce is genuinely yours.

Not "yours" in the legal sense. Yours in the sense that you directed the thinking, made the judgment calls, and can defend the result.

---

## The Problem ESF Solves

You use AI to draft something. The output reads well. Then you realize you cannot point to which ideas are yours and which you accepted because they sounded right.

This is not a discipline problem. It is a structural one. Research on metacognition shows that polished AI output triggers a sense of comprehension even when the reader did not produce the reasoning behind it (Tankelevitch et al., 2024). The better AI writes, the harder it becomes to distinguish your position from the AI's framing.

Three common responses fail to address this:

1. **Banning or permitting AI** answers whether you can use it, not how to maintain ownership when you do.
2. **Focusing only on students** ignores that professionals using AI in their own work face the same drift problem.
3. **Requiring disclosure** documents that AI was involved but does not address what happened during the work. You can disclose every AI contribution while having exercised no intellectual direction over the result.

The real question is never "was AI used?" It is "whose thinking is this?"

## How ESF Works

ESF was built from practice, not theory. Nathan Madrid developed its core constructs through daily use, integrating AI into curriculum development, assessment design, and institutional work at an art and design university. He built them because he kept encountering the same problem: AI-assisted drafts that drifted from his intent in ways that were difficult to detect after the fact.

The literature review (35 sources across 8 research areas) came after the constructs existed. It confirmed that the problems ESF addresses are real and documented. But the solutions came from practice first.

### The Core Idea: State Your Position Before AI

Before you open any AI tool on a project, write down what you think. Your stance, what matters most, what you will not compromise on. This takes five to ten minutes. Those minutes are where the real thinking happens.

ESF calls this a Position Statement: capture your intellectual direction before AI offers its version. Once AI gives you a polished draft, your own idea becomes nearly impossible to separate from what the AI proposed. The written position preserves your thinking in its uncontaminated form.

### The Five Questions

At every significant decision point, run the Five Questions: Can I defend this? Is this mine? Did I verify? Would I teach this? Is my disclosure honest? If any answer is no, stop and fix it. The full questions with context are in [WORKFLOW.md](../WORKFLOW.md).

Question 2 catches most people. After an hour of working with AI on a draft, the line between "my position" and "a position I accepted because the AI said it well" blurs fast. That is not a weakness. It is how polished output works on your thinking.

### Scaling to the Stakes

Not everything needs the full process. ESF uses a concept called Content Epistemic Weight to calibrate effort to stakes: High-weight work (original arguments, portfolio pieces, client deliverables) requires the full process. Medium-weight work (briefs, proposals) needs an abbreviated position and key checkpoints. Low-weight work (emails, scheduling, formatting) needs light review and attribution. The full table is in [WORKFLOW.md](../WORKFLOW.md).

The classification is based on how much human thinking the content demands, not how much AI a policy permits. A portfolio piece is high-weight because the creative and intellectual claims must originate with you. A meeting agenda is low-weight because it makes no intellectual claims.

### Records of Resistance

When you reject, revise, or deliberately override AI output, write it down: what the AI suggested, what you chose instead, and why. These take thirty seconds each.

Records of Resistance are evidence of judgment. Every time you catch AI pulling your work toward something generic, predictable, or wrong, and you redirect it, that decision is the skill. Over the course of a project, your Records of Resistance become the clearest proof that you were directing the process, not following it.

## The Companion Workflow

The ESF Companion uses a single five-phase workflow:

```
1. INQUIRE     Read the brief. Ask what you already know,
               what you are uncertain about, what you assume.
       |
2. POSITION    Write your Position Statement. No AI yet.
       |
3. EXPLORE     Bring AI in as a thinking partner.
               It challenges your position, surfaces alternatives.
               It does not originate direction.
       |
4. MAKE        Work with AI on the deliverable.
               Log AI contributions. Record resistance.
               Run the Five Questions at each major section.
       |
5. REFLECT     Compare final work to your Position Statement.
               Write an honest disclosure statement.
               Attach it to the finished work.
```

The Position Statement is the gate. Everything downstream depends on it. Without it, you have no anchor for evaluating whether AI output serves your intent or replaces it.

## For Educators

The ESF Companion is the same tool for everyone. Students, faculty, professionals, and independent creators all use the same core loop: Position, Work, Monitor, Surface, Decide. The difference for educators is what you do with it.

When the Companion detects that you are an educator during onboarding, it introduces two ways to work:

- **Your own practice.** You use the Companion the same way anyone does. Write a Position Statement before AI enters your work. Monitor for drift. Record resistance. Run the Five Questions. The problem ESF solves applies to faculty producing syllabi, assessments, and institutional documents, not only to students producing coursework.
- **Your students' environment.** You author project briefs with ESF requirements embedded in frontmatter. Those briefs configure the Companion for your students: whether the Position Statement is required, optional, or skipped; whether the Five Questions enforce hard stops or surface observations; minimum Records of Resistance per project. When you design a brief, you are designing the epistemic environment your students work within.

These are not separate systems. They are the same Companion configured for different purposes. The constructs (Position Statement, Five Questions, Content Weight, Records of Resistance, Disclosure Protocol) are built into the Companion. They do not require a separate framework or installation.

For institutional adoption, including forking the repository, setting course-specific requirements, and distributing to students, see [Adopting ESF for Your Institution](institutional-adoption.md).

## Why Tool-Agnostic?

ESF does not recommend, require, or assume any specific AI tool. This is a design decision. The core problem (maintaining intellectual ownership when AI assists in producing work) occurs with every generative AI system. Writing a clear Position Statement, detecting drift, verifying claims, and disclosing honestly are durable skills that transfer across any tool, model generation, and discipline.

The question is never "which AI did you use?" The question is "can you defend this as yours?"

## Research Basis

ESF is grounded in 35 verified scholarly sources. Four findings shaped the framework most directly:

1. **Epistemic agency requires structural support.** Cognitive tools extend thinking legitimately only when the human actively endorses their contributions (Clark & Chalmers, 1998). Without a documented pre-drafting position, endorsement reduces to "I didn't object."
2. **Metacognitive monitoring fails under fluency.** Catching subtle misrepresentation in AI output takes real effort, and failures of monitoring are common even among experienced professionals (Tankelevitch et al., 2024).
3. **Human-first sequencing develops agency.** Students (and professionals) must establish their own understanding before AI enters the process. Socratic AI tutoring that questions reasoning produces stronger epistemic agency than AI that answers directly (Degen & Asanov, 2025).
4. **Widespread AI adoption without structural safeguards produces "organized immaturity":** people become passive consumers of AI-generated knowledge rather than active producers and evaluators of it (Lindebaum et al., 2025).

The literature review and citations are documented in the ESF research files within this repository.
