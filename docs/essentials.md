# ESF Essentials: Three Practices for AI-Assisted Work

You use AI in your work. The question is whether the work it helps you produce is actually yours.

The Epistemic Stewardship Framework (ESF) is a set of practices for working with AI while keeping your thinking, your judgment, and your direction at the center. It is not a restriction on AI use. It is a structure that makes your AI use better.

Three practices. Learn them. Use them every time.

---

## 1. State Your Position Before AI

Before you open any AI tool on a project, write a Position Statement: a short document (200 to 400 words, rough is fine) that captures three things.

- **Your position.** What do you want to make, argue, or explore?
- **What matters most.** What matters most? What should you avoid?
- **Your non-negotiables.** What must be present in the final work, no matter what the AI suggests?

This takes five to ten minutes. Those minutes are where the real thinking happens. Your Position Statement becomes the anchor for every decision that follows. When AI output drifts from your intent, the statement is how you notice.

---

## 2. Ask the Five Questions at Every Checkpoint

From the moment AI enters your process, apply these questions at every significant decision point: after each working session, after completing a section, before submitting.

1. **Can I defend this?** Could I explain every part of my work without saying "the AI suggested it"?
2. **Is this mine?** Did I direct this, or did I accept the AI's framing because it sounded reasonable? "Mine" means you exercised design authority, not that you wrote every word. The test: did the AI perform the judgment through which your professional knowledge develops? If so, you have given up more than authorship.
3. **Did I verify?** Have I checked facts, sources, and references independently?
4. **Would I teach this?** Could I explain my reasoning to a colleague or collaborator?
5. **Is my disclosure honest?** Does my description of AI use match what actually happened?

If any answer is no, you have three options: revise the work, reduce the AI contribution, or disclose honestly. All three are acceptable. Ignoring the question is not.

---

## 3. Document When You Push Back

A Record of Resistance captures any moment you reject, revise, or deliberately override AI output. The format is simple:

- **What the AI suggested**
- **What you chose instead**
- **Why** (referencing your Position Statement)

Records of Resistance are not busywork. They are evidence of creative judgment. Every time you catch the AI pulling your work toward something generic, predictable, or wrong, and you redirect it, that decision is the skill.

Document it. It takes thirty seconds. Over the course of a project, your Records of Resistance become the clearest proof that you were directing the process, not following it.

---

## The Short Version

State your position. Check your work. Document your decisions.

That is epistemic stewardship. It works with any AI tool, any discipline, any project. Start now.
